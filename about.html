Why this exists

When a conflict breaks out, the information you receive depends almost entirely on where you live and what language you speak. A citizen in Moscow and a citizen in Kyiv are consuming almost entirely different realities — not because one is lying and the other is telling the truth, but because all media carries perspective, and most people only ever see one.

ConflictLens was built on a simple premise: if you can see both narratives side by side, you become a better-informed reader. You start to notice the silences — what each side chooses not to say. You notice the framing — which deaths are mourned, which are justified. You notice the language — one side's "terrorist" is another side's "resistance fighter."

"The first casualty of war is not truth — it's the acknowledgment that truth has multiple faces."

— ConflictLens editorial principle

This is not a project of false equivalence. We are not saying that all media is equally credible, or that all sides in a conflict have equal moral standing. We show press freedom scores, funding sources, and editorial affiliations precisely because context matters. We trust you to weigh that context.

// 02

Our principles

ConflictLens operates by a set of editorial principles that guide every decision — which conflicts to cover, which sources to include, and how to present them.

01
Radical transparency
Every source is labeled with its funding model, editorial affiliation, and press freedom score. We hide nothing about where the information comes from.
02
No editorial voice
ConflictLens does not write analysis, op-eds, or commentary. We aggregate. The synthesis is yours to make.
03
Asymmetry is visible
When one side has a free press and the other has state propaganda, we show that clearly. Transparency about asymmetry is itself a form of journalism.
04
No ads, no agenda
ConflictLens is an independent open-source project. It is not funded by any government, NGO, or corporation. No advertiser influences what we show.
05
Primary sources first
We link directly to the original media outlet. We do not paraphrase, summarize, or editorialize their content. You read their words, not ours.
06
Open and evolving
The source list is not final. We welcome critique of our source selection. The code is open source. Anyone can audit what we do and why.
// 03

What we are not

Defining what ConflictLens is not is as important as defining what it is.

✕ Not a news outlet. We do not produce original journalism. We aggregate and present existing coverage from established media organizations.
✕ Not a fact-checker. We do not verify the accuracy of individual claims made by the sources we feature. That verification is your responsibility as a reader.
✕ Not neutral in the sense of having no values. We believe in press freedom, transparency, and the public's right to information. Those are values. But we do not take sides in the conflicts we cover.
✕ Not a platform for disinformation. We feature state media with explicit warnings about their editorial independence. Showing propaganda with a label is different from endorsing it.
✓ A tool for media literacy. ConflictLens is, above all, an exercise in teaching people to read news critically — to ask who wrote this, who funded it, and what they chose not to say.
// 04

How we measure press freedom

The press freedom scores shown on each source card come from the Reporters Without Borders (RSF) World Press Freedom Index, an independent annual ranking that evaluates media freedom in 180 countries. We use this as a proxy for editorial independence — not as the final word on a media outlet's credibility.

A low RSF score for a country does not mean every story from that country is false. It means the media environment operates under constraints — legal, political, or economic — that may affect editorial decisions.

RSF Press Freedom Scale — 0 to 100
Good (75–100) Satisfactory (55–74) Problematic (40–54) Difficult (25–39) Very serious (0–24)

Source: Reporters Without Borders — rsf.org. Data updated annually. ConflictLens uses the most recent available index.

Open source project

ConflictLens is fully open source. The entire codebase is available on GitHub. You can audit every line, suggest new sources, report errors, or fork the project to create your own version.

This transparency is intentional. A project about media transparency should itself be transparent.
